Good metric, bad metric

2026-02-11 · 5 min read · PM Data

A short field guide to picking metrics that actually move product decisions, not just dashboards.

Every product team has a metrics problem, and almost none of them know it. The ones that do recognize it have usually graduated past the symptoms — the dashboards no one looks at, the OKRs that don’t change behavior, the weekly reviews that become rituals — and started asking the harder question: which of these numbers, if it moved, would change what we decide to do tomorrow?

That’s the bar I want to set: a metric is only useful if it can plausibly change a decision. Everything else is decoration.

The three questions

Before I adopt any new metric — whether it’s for an OKR, a dashboard, or a single experiment — I run it through three questions:

Can I name the decision it informs? Not "improve user engagement," not "track health." A specific, near-term call I’ll have to make.

Is it directional, or is it absolute? "Active users" is absolute and almost always misleading. "Active users week-over-week, normalized for marketing spend" is directional.

Could the metric move without the underlying thing actually moving? This is the goodhart trap. If your metric can be gamed by surface changes, then improvements to the metric will routinely come without the user actually being better off.

The patterns that consistently fail, in my experience: vanity counters with no denominator, retention metrics measured on too short a window, and any metric that survives a re-org because nobody’s sure what it means anymore.

What good looks like

The teams I’ve seen do this well share a few things. They have a small dashboard — usually fewer than ten metrics — and they review it religiously. They retire metrics aggressively when they stop being useful. And critically, they’re willing to say "we don’t measure that yet" instead of inventing a proxy and committing to it.

If you can’t do all three, you don’t have a metrics problem. You have a decision-making problem dressed up in numbers.
